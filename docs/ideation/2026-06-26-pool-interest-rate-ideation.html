This revision reframes the Pool around a new design hypothesis: the Pool's single rate functions as a reference ceiling on the whole market. If the Pool offers 10%, no Book borrower accepts a lend offer priced worse than that and instead routes to the Pool, so market rates cluster at or below the Pool rate. The Pool is a finite reference anchor, not an infinite always-on counterparty, which keeps the ceiling soft and the Book relevant once Pool capital is consumed. That insight inverts the captured “Pool prices off the Book” assumption and reorganizes the rate-setting ideas below.

What changed in this revision

The prior “Pool reads the Book” ideas (snapshot, capacity-weighted TWAP) are superseded — the ceiling reframing reverses the dependency direction (the Pool now anchors the Book). The prior “per-loan inherited APR” idea is subsumed: the Pool is single-rate so it can be a legible ceiling, and per-order rates become a Book behavior that floats below that ceiling. The admin-constant, canonical-base-rate, and staleness-fallback ideas are carried forward, promoted or reframed. Superseded/subsumed prior survivors are recorded in the Rejection Summary so the trail is visible.

Grounding Context

Codebase context. Foundry/Solidity. The Book (OVRFLOBook) is active scope; the Pool (OVRFLOPool) is deferred and must attach with zero change to src/OVRFLO.sol, reading the factory only as a read-only provenance registry. The deliberate design today is that “the book and pool have no such relationship — they only read provenance.”

Shared pricing primitive. StreamPricing is a pure library shared by Book and Pool. A single annualized rate aprBps term-adjusts per stream: factor = 1 + apr·ttm/(YEAR·BPS). “The rate” is an annualized APR, the same units the Book uses.

How the Book sets rate. APR is per-order, bounded by [aprMinBps, aprMaxBps] under an immutable APR_MAX_CEILING. Launch locks both bounds to 1000 (10%); the multisig widens later. Bounds are economic guardrails, not safety invariants. Directionally, a borrower always prefers a lower lend APR, so a standing cheaper venue caps the lend-side rate from above.

Pool spec (deferred). Closed-end, sealed, pro-rata. Lifecycle Open → ClosedToDeposits → Sealed → Settled with two levers (amount cap closes deposits; time deadline seals), lazy seal folded into the first post-deadline claim, balance-checked borrow as the entire imbalance-prevention mechanism, flat shares = underlying deposited, and totalObligations = Σ obligations as the frozen lender entitlement. The captured intent: “the pool launches after the book, because it will price off the book's market-set APR rather than a constant.”

New constraint (this session)

The Pool rate is a reference rate that economically caps the market, not a live, infinite, always-on counterparty. Because the Pool is closed-end and finite, the ceiling is soft: it anchors the market while funded, and the Book reabsorbs demand once the Pool is sealed or exhausted. This both motivates the ceiling and bounds how literally it can be enforced.

Project rules in scope. Prefer off-chain multisig verification over redundant on-chain checks; favor simplicity over abstraction; stay Pendle-specific; no keeper/bounty; no OVRFLO.sol change.

Topic Axes

  • Rate-setting & the ceiling anchor — how the Pool's single rate is chosen and published as the reference everyone quotes against.
  • Ceiling mechanism — how the Pool rate actually caps the Book: on-chain enforcement vs purely behavioral/arbitrage.
  • Book/Pool dependency direction — who leads whom; resolving the “Pool prices off Book” vs “Pool anchors Book” tension and the cold-start order.
  • Lifecycle, capacity & sealing — how finiteness, caps, and seal timing make the ceiling soft and keep the Book alive.
  • Economic guardrails — the floor under the ceiling (lender participation, aprMin), fallbacks, and manipulation resistance.

1

Reverse the dependency: Pool leads, Book follows (Pool-as-anchor)

Book/Pool dependency direction Confidence 72% Complexity Medium
Description
The captured spec has the Pool launch after the Book so it can “price off the book's market-set APR.” The ceiling insight inverts this: the Pool's single governance-set rate is the anchor the Book discovers around and under. Launch the Pool first (or concurrently) with a credible rate so it seeds the thin early Book, rather than waiting for a Book that has no trustworthy rate yet. This resolves the chicken-and-egg — “Pool needs the Book's rate” vs “Book has no rate until it has liquidity.”
Axis
Book/Pool dependency direction
Basis
direct: spec — “the pool launches after the book, because it will price off the book's market-set APR rather than a constant”; this idea deliberately reverses that premise. reasoned: a thin Book's rate is noisy and manipulable (the prior revision's core concern), so it cannot be the anchor at launch — something must seed the reference, and a governance-set Pool rate is the natural seed.
Rationale
Makes the ceiling actually function as the organizing reference and fixes the bootstrap ordering. Once the direction is flipped, every “Pool reads the Book” mechanism becomes unnecessary, collapsing a lot of complexity.
Downsides
Contradicts a captured requirement, so it needs explicit sign-off; puts the burden of a good anchor rate on governance; a wrong anchor mis-prices the whole market (mitigated by competing pools and the immutable bounds).
2

Pool rate as the Book's on-chain aprMaxBps (hard ceiling)

Ceiling mechanism Confidence 60% Complexity Medium–High
Description
Rather than the multisig hand-setting aprMaxBps, the Book reads the active Pool's rate for a market and uses it as the dynamic upper APR bound on lend-side posts. No lend offer can be posted above the Pool rate, so the ceiling is enforced in code, not just by behavior. Per-order Book rates still vary — they simply float below this clamp (this subsumes the prior “per-loan inherited APR” idea). When no Pool is active, fall back to the governance aprMaxBps (idea #8).
Axis
Ceiling mechanism
Basis
direct: the Book already enforces aprMinBps ≤ apr ≤ aprMaxBps per order under an immutable ceiling; swapping the static aprMaxBps for a Pool read is a small, well-scoped change to an existing check. reasoned: a borrower never accepts a lend APR above the Pool's, so offers above it are dead capital — clamping at post time makes that explicit and removes wasted gas and UX confusion.
Rationale
Turns the user's insight into a guarantee instead of a hope: the market literally cannot price the lend side above the ceiling.
Downsides
Couples Book to Pool state, the dependency the spec deliberately avoided; Pool-rate changes/staleness need handling; cleanly bounds only the lend side (the sale side needs separate treatment); adds Book surface. Sits in tension with the “reference rate, not a live counterparty” clarification — this is the strongest-enforcement option and should be weighed against idea #3.
3

Behavioral / arbitrage ceiling — no on-chain coupling (minimal default)

Ceiling mechanism Confidence 80% Complexity Low
Description
Keep Book and Pool fully decoupled. The Pool is simply an attractive, fixed-rate, finite venue; rational borrowers route to whichever is cheaper, so the market rate naturally clusters at or below the Pool rate without any contract reading another. The ceiling is an emergent economic property, not an enforced invariant.
Axis
Ceiling mechanism
Basis
direct: project rule — “prefer off-chain verification over redundant on-chain checks” — and the spec's deliberate decoupling (“the book and pool have no such relationship”). reasoned: arbitrage enforces the ceiling for free as long as the Pool is a real, accessible venue, which is exactly the “reference rate, not a live counterparty” model the user described.
Rationale
Zero added coupling, honors the session's clarification most directly, simplest to ship and audit. This is the conservative default the others should beat to justify their extra surface.
Downsides
The ceiling is soft — when the Pool is sealed or exhausted the Book can exceed it; relies on borrowers actually routing optimally (UI/aggregator dependent); no on-chain guarantee for integrators.
4

Single admin/governance rate per pool, immutable for life

Rate-setting & ceiling anchor Confidence 85% Complexity Low
Description
createPool(market, aprBps, …) takes one fixed aprBps (validated to bounds); every loan in that pool prices at it for the pool's life. This single rate is what the market reads as the ceiling reference. Different pools carry different anchor rates; selection and competition across pools refine the reference over time.
Axis
Rate-setting & ceiling anchor
Basis
direct: the Book's launch posture (aprMinBps = aprMaxBps = 1000 → only 10% legal) applied per pool; createPool is already on the deferred surface.
Rationale
A ceiling needs one legible number. A per-pool constant is the simplest credible anchor and the natural unit the market quotes against — it is the value ideas #1, #2, and #5 all reference.
Downsides
Relies on governance/creators picking sane rates; a stale pool drifts from the live market until a new pool is created.
5

Interest-rate corridor: Pool = ceiling, aprMin = floor

Economic guardrails Confidence 65% Complexity Low
Description
Model the system as a central-bank-style rate corridor. The Pool rate is the ceiling (borrowers won't pay more); aprMinBps is the floor (offers can't post below). The Book trades within the corridor. The floor is load-bearing because the ceiling can't be pushed arbitrarily low: below some rate, lenders stop supplying the Pool, so lender participation endogenously bounds where the ceiling can sit.
Axis
Economic guardrails
Basis
external: central-bank standing-facility design — a deposit facility sets the floor, a lending facility sets the ceiling, and the interbank rate trades between them. reasoned: at Pool rate → 0, lenders earn nothing and supply dries up, so the ceiling is naturally bounded below by participation; this explains why “set the ceiling low” has a hard limit.
Rationale
Gives governance a clean mental model and exactly two knobs (ceiling = Pool rate, floor = aprMin) that fully describe the rate environment, and explains the lower bound on the ceiling.
Downsides
The corridor only holds while the Pool is funded; mapping monetary-policy intuition onto a closed-end finite pool is imperfect (there is no infinite facility), so it is a framing aid, not a literal mechanism.
6

Finiteness is the safety valve — size capacity & sealing to keep the Book alive

Lifecycle, capacity & sealing Confidence 68% Complexity Low–Medium
Description
Flip the constraint: if the Pool had infinite capital at its rate, it would be the entire market and the Book would die. The Pool's closed-end cap and seal deadline are precisely what keep the ceiling soft and the Book relevant — when Pool capital is consumed, demand spills back to the Book. So treat Pool capacity and seal timing as deliberate market-design levers, not just accounting bounds.
Axis
Lifecycle, capacity & sealing
Basis
direct: the spec's two lifecycle levers (“amount cap closes deposits; time deadline seals”) and “balance-checked borrow … the entire imbalance-prevention mechanism.” reasoned: a finite venue can only anchor, not clear, the whole market — that finiteness is the feature behind the user's “reference rate, not always-on counterparty.”
Rationale
Reframes capacity and seal parameters as the dial balancing “strong anchor” against “leave room for Book price discovery,” which is the lever that makes the whole ceiling model work in practice.
Downsides
Requires governance to actively tune capacity per market; too small a pool is a weak anchor, too large starves the Book.
7

Pool publishes a canonical “OVRFLO base rate” consumed everywhere

Rate-setting & ceiling anchor Confidence 62% Complexity Medium
Description
Expose the Pool's anchor rate as a single canonical baseApr(market) read in the shared StreamPricing layer, consumed by the Book (as ceiling/clamp in #2), by quote/UX surfaces, and by any future product (fixed-rate notes, structured products). One source of truth for “the OVRFLO rate.”
Axis
Rate-setting & ceiling anchor
Basis
direct: StreamPricing is already “a new library … shared by book and pool.” reasoned: a single published reference is what makes the ceiling legible and reusable; centralizing it once gives every consumer the same number and the same governance hook.
Rationale
Compounding leverage: makes ideas #2 and #5 implementations of one primitive, and lets future products inherit the anchor for free.
Downsides
Premature generality if only the Book ever consumes it (tension with the simplicity rule); still requires choosing how the base rate is derived — idea #4 supplies that.
8

Book aprMaxBps fallback when no Pool is active

Economic guardrails Confidence 78% Complexity Low
Description
Any design where the Book reads the Pool as its ceiling (#2/#7) needs defined behavior when no Pool is active for a market — none created yet, or all pools sealed/settled between cohorts. The fallback: use the governance-set static aprMaxBps, and only use the Pool-derived ceiling when a live pool exists.
Axis
Economic guardrails
Basis
direct: pools are closed-end and will routinely sit between cohorts (sealed/settled with none open). reasoned: a ceiling source that can be absent must have a fallback, or Book posting bricks in exactly the gaps the Pool is meant to anchor.
Rationale
Cheap, decisive safety net that makes the on-chain ceiling (#2) shippable across the Pool's natural lifecycle gaps.
Downsides
A companion idea, not standalone; introduces an “is a pool active?” check and a second code path to test.

Rejection Summary

#IdeaReason rejected / superseded
1Live best-offer Book read at every borrow Manipulable per-borrow, per-borrow read gas, undefined when no offer rests — and moot under the ceiling reframing (Pool no longer reads the Book).
2Uniform-price sealed-bid auction at seal (T-bill style) Lifecycle mismatch — borrows happen during Open and need a rate before seal; heavy on-chain machinery violates the simplicity rule.
3In-pool lender min-APR order book (fill lowest first) Duplicates the Book's job and breaks the flat shares = underlying pro-rata model; also conflicts with the single-rate ceiling.
4Utilization-curve rate (Aave/Compound style) Circular — a borrow needs its rate before seal, but final utilization is only known at seal; live reads are gameable by deposit timing.
5“min APR = 0 is safe” as a standalone idea A supporting property of the bounds, not a mechanism; folded into the grounding and into idea #5's floor argument.
6Per-loan inherited APR as a Pool property (prior survivor #1) Subsumed: the ceiling reframing makes the Pool single-rate so it can be a legible reference; per-order rates are now a Book behavior floating below the Pool ceiling (idea #2).
7Pool reads a Book snapshot at createPool (prior survivor #3) Superseded: the ceiling reframing reverses the dependency direction (Pool anchors Book), and a thin early Book has no trustworthy rate to read at cold-start.
8Pool reads capacity-weighted Book TWAP (prior survivor #5) Superseded by the same direction reversal; also over-engineered relative to a governance-set anchor.
9Pool as an infinite, always-on backstop counterparty Contradicts the session clarification (“reference rate, not a live always-on counterparty”) and is impossible for a closed-end finite pool.
10Re-specify lifecycle/accounting (lazy seal, three-number gate, pro-rata shares) Already pinned in the requirements doc; not open design space for this ideation.
11Set Pool rate → 0 to maximize the ceiling effect Degenerate: destroys lender supply, so no pool exists to anchor anything (this is exactly idea #5's floor argument).
